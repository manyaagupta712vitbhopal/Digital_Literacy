**Case Study: Phishing — The 2016 Google & Facebook Invoice Scam**

_What It Is?_
Phishing is a cyberattack in which criminals impersonate trusted entities — such as banks, established companies, or known colleagues — to deceive victims into surrendering sensitive information or transferring money. It relies primarily on psychological manipulation rather than technical hacking.

_How It Happened?_
Impersonation: Lithuanian national Evaldas Rimasauskas registered a company in Latvia bearing the same name as Quanta Computer, a legitimate Taiwan-based hardware supplier that both Google and Facebook regularly used.
Forged Documents: He fabricated invoices, contracts, and official letters complete with forged signatures of real executives at both companies.
Email Deception: Fraudulent emails were sent directly to finance department employees, requesting payment for services that appeared entirely routine.
Money Transferred: Employees, recognising the familiar vendor name and convinced by the professional paperwork, wired over $100 million across multiple transactions into overseas accounts.
Laundering: Funds were rapidly dispersed across banks in Latvia, Cyprus, Slovakia, Lithuania, Hungary, and Hong Kong to evade detection.

_Who Was Targeted?_
Finance and accounting staff at two of the world's most sophisticated technology companies — proving that phishing exploits human trust, not just technical vulnerabilities.
Consequences
Rimasauskas was extradited to the United States and pleaded guilty to wire fraud in 2019. He received a five-year federal prison sentence. Google recovered most of its losses; Facebook recovered a portion. Both companies subsequently overhauled their vendor payment verification processes entirely.

**Source: U.S. Department of Justice, United States v. Evaldas Rimasauskas, 2019. justice.gov**
